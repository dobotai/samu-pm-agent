# **Part 1: Overview and Your Role**

## **What KS Media Does**

KS Media is a YouTube content agency that produces long-form YouTube videos for B2B clients. We handle the full pipeline \- from video ideas and scripts to editing, thumbnails, and publishing. We currently work with 40+ clients.

## **Your Role: Ops Manager**

You are the operational backbone of the agency. You make sure every video moves through the pipeline on time and nothing falls through the cracks. You do not create content yourself \- you manage the people and processes that do.

## **Your Responsibilities**

* Assigning client recordings to editors and thumbnail designers in Airtable

* Managing editors \- monitoring progress, tracking deadlines, following up, handling questions

* Managing client communication \- responding within 20 minutes, sending videos for review, following up on recordings and feedback

* Sending approved videos to clients with all deliverables

* Scheduling videos on YouTube

* Handling editor payments twice a month (15th and 30th)

* Onboarding new editors

* Keeping Airtable statuses accurate and up to date at all times

* Daily sweeps to make sure nothing is stuck anywhere in the pipeline

# **Part 2: Understanding the Systems**

## **Airtable**

Airtable is our project management database. It is where every video is tracked from script to published.

### **Structure**

The database has three main tabs:

* **Clients** \- All current and archived clients. Grouped by active (clients we currently work with) and archived (clients we no longer work with). Each client record includes whether we write scripts for them, their billing info, and a **Deliverables** field that lists exactly what each client receives (thumbnail, social posts, etc.).

* **Teams** \- All editors and the thumbnail designer. Includes their name, role (long-form editor, short-form editor, thumbnail designer), email, Slack ID, and Slack channel ID.

* **Videos** \- This is where all the work happens. Every video is a separate entry with fields for client, video number, format, assigned editor, deadline, editing status, raw footage link, edited footage link, link to script, thumbnail deadline, thumbnail status, and client notes.

### **Editing Statuses**

The editing status field is how we track where every video is in the pipeline. See Part 3 for the full status table and what each one means.

### **Views**

Airtable views are filtered and grouped versions of the Videos tab. They let you quickly see videos at a specific stage or from a specific angle. Here are the views you will use:

**Status-based views (videos at a specific stage):** \- New Videos \- Input Needed From Client (Scripting) \- Needs Scripting \- Script Needs Checking (QC) \- Scripting Process \- Needs Social Post \- Video Needs Checking (QC) \- Editing Revisions \- Approved by Agency \- Sent to Client for Checking \- Needs Posting

**Overview views (broader perspective):** \- Videos This Month (LF) \- all long-form videos this month. Use this when assigning videos. \- Videos in the Last 60 Days \- all recent videos grouped by client. Good for getting the big picture. \- Videos This Month (SF) \- short-form videos this month. \- Videos by Editors \- all active videos grouped by editor with deadlines. Use this to check editor workload. \- Editing Deadlines \- calendar view of upcoming deadlines.

You can also create your own custom views with whatever filters and groupings are useful for your workflow.

### **How to Create a Custom View**

1. Click the “+” or “Create new view” button in the sidebar.

2. Choose “Grid” as the view type.

3. Name it something descriptive.

4. Use the Filter, Group, and Sort options to customize what you see.

5. Use the Hide Fields option to show only the fields you need.

## **Google Drive**

Google Drive is where all client files are stored \- raw footage, scripts, finished videos, thumbnails, and other assets.

### **Folder Structure**

Each client has a main folder. Inside the main folder:

* **A subfolder that the client has access to.** This is the folder the client can see. Inside it:

  * **Raw Footage** \- Where clients upload their recordings.

  * **Finished Videos** \- Where you create subfolders for each completed video and upload the thumbnail and deliverables. Each video gets its own numbered subfolder (e.g., “62”).

  * **Thumbnail Photos** \- Branding assets and thumbnail reference photos the client has uploaded.

  * **Scripts** \- A video ideas document and monthly subfolders with scripts (e.g., Month 1, Month 2, etc.). Each month typically has 4 scripts.

* **Files outside the client-accessible subfolder (internal only).** This is where you will find:

  * **Client Information** document \- contains all client info we have on file.

  * **Description Template** (if available) \- the YouTube description template for that client.

  * Other internal files.

**Important:** The client can only see the subfolder that is shared with them. They cannot see the main folder or internal files.

### **Exceptions**

Most clients follow this structure, but there are a couple of exceptions where the folder setup is slightly different. These are rare \- 90% of clients follow the standard structure.

# **Part 3: The Video Production Pipeline**

This section walks you through the full lifecycle of a video at KS Media \- from the initial idea to a published YouTube video. Understanding this pipeline is critical because every task you do as the Ops Manager connects to a specific stage in this process.

Read this section first. The detailed how-to instructions for each of your tasks are covered in later sections, but this gives you the full picture so you always know where a video stands and what needs to happen next.

---

## **The Full Pipeline**

Below is every stage a video goes through at KS Media. Your responsibilities as Ops Manager are marked with **(You)**. Other stages are handled by Samu, the scriptwriter, the quality checker, or the thumbnail designer \- but you need to understand the full flow so you can track progress and catch bottlenecks.

### **Stage 1: Ideation**

**Who:** Samu **What happens:** An automated Slack ping reminds Samu that a client needs new video ideas (this fires 5 days before the client’s next billing date). Samu creates 4 video ideas and sends them to the client for feedback.

### **Stage 2: Script Preparation**

**Who:** Scriptwriter (with Samu doing script QC)

Once the client approves the ideas:

1. The scriptwriter creates 4 new items in Airtable, one per video idea, and sets their editing status to “Sent to Scriptwriter.”

2. The scriptwriter writes the scripts.

3. Samu QCs the scripts before they go to the client.

4. The scriptwriter sends the finished scripts to the client for review.

5. The scriptwriter handles any script revision requests from the client.

At this point, the videos exist in Airtable but have no assigned editor and no deadline \- they are waiting for the client to record.

### **Stage 3: Client Records the Video**

**Who:** The client \+ You (follow-up) **What happens:** Once scripts are finished and sent to the client, you should get an ETA from the client on when they plan to record. Follow up on this to make sure we stay on pace with the content calendar.

The client records the video based on the approved script. They will either: \- Post the recording link in their Slack channel (most clients do this), or \- Upload the raw footage directly to their Google Drive raw footage folder. In this case, the client will still let you know in Slack that they uploaded it.

**Your job:** Monitor Slack channels to catch new recordings as they come in. You do not need to check Google Drive daily \- clients will notify you in Slack when footage is uploaded there. When a recording arrives, move immediately to Stage 4\.

### **Stage 4: Assign the Video to an Editor and Thumbnail Designer**

**Who:** You **What happens:** This is one of your core tasks. When a client records a video, you go to Airtable and set up the video entry. Here is the order of fields to fill in:

1. Find the matching video entry in Airtable (or create one if it does not exist yet). Match it to the correct script by checking the script folder or the video number.

2. Set the **Assigned Editor** \- choose the editor who edits for that client.

3. Set the **Deadline** \- standard turnaround is 3 days from assignment. Check the “Videos by Editors” view to see if the editor is already working on other videos and adjust accordingly.

4. Set the **Editing Status** to “Sent to Editor” \- this triggers an automated Slack notification to the assigned editor in their channel.

5. Paste the **Raw Footage** link in the raw footage field.

6. Paste the **Link to Script** if not already there.

7. Set the **Thumbnail Deadline** \- always set this to the next day after assignment (we have a 24-hour thumbnail turnaround).

8. Set the **Thumbnail Status** to “New” \- this triggers an automated notification to the thumbnail designer (Ram).

9. Add any **Client Notes** \- if the client left instructions, context, or special requests in Slack when they sent the recording, copy those into the Client Notes field so the editor can see them.

**Edge cases:** \- If there are multiple recording links (e.g., separate intro/outro files), paste them in the Client Notes field since the raw footage field only holds one link. Note in Client Notes that the editor should look there for all files. \- For urgent videos that need to jump the queue, set a shorter deadline and message the editor directly explaining the priority shift.

*Detailed step-by-step instructions are in Part 4\.*

### **Stage 5: Editor Works on the Video**

**Who:** Editor (editing) \+ You (monitoring) **What happens:** The editor works on the video according to the deadline. Automated daily check-in messages go out to each editor asking for progress updates.

**Your job during this stage:** \- Monitor editor Slack channels for check-in responses and questions. \- If an editor has a question you can answer (e.g., they need an asset from the video, a clarification about the recording), handle it. \- If an editor has a question only Samu can answer (e.g., about video style, pricing, creative preferences), flag it to Samu. \- Track whether videos are on pace to meet their deadlines. If an editor’s update suggests they will miss the deadline, follow up to understand why, get a realistic delivery date, and adjust the deadline in Airtable. \- If an editor does not respond to the daily check-in within **10 hours**, send a follow-up message in their Slack channel. \- If they still do not respond within **24 hours** of the original check-in, text them on WhatsApp: *Hey, this is \[YOUR NAME\] from KS Media. Is everything ok? We haven’t got an update from you on Slack for almost a day. Please check in ASAP.*

*Detailed instructions on managing editors are in Part 5\.*

### **Stage 6: Thumbnail Creation and Approval**

**Who:** Thumbnail designer (Ram) \+ Samu (review) **What happens:** Ram creates the thumbnail and posts it in his Slack channel as a thread. Samu reviews the thumbnail and provides feedback \- this usually takes a few rounds of revisions. Ram updates the thumbnail status in Airtable himself throughout this process.

Once Samu approves the thumbnail, Samu will tag you in the Slack thread. You do not need to download the thumbnail at this point. When you later need it (to send the video to the client or to schedule on YouTube), you will:

1. Go to Ram’s Slack channel.

2. Search the channel for the specific client/video thumbnail.

3. Download it at that point.

4. Upload it where it is needed (Google Drive finished videos folder or YouTube).

*Detailed instructions are in Part 7\.*

### **Stage 7: Quality Check (QC)**

**Who:** Quality Checker **What happens:** When the editor finishes the video, they paste the edited footage link (Frame.io) in Airtable and update the editing status to “Submitted for QC.”

The quality checker reviews the video and either: \- **Approves it** \- the status is updated to “Approved by Agency” and the video moves to Stage 8\. \- **Sends revisions** \- the editing status is set to “Editing Revisions,” and the editor is notified in Slack with the specific changes needed. The editor implements the revisions and resubmits. This loop repeats until the quality checker approves.

**Your job:** You are not doing the QC yourself, but you are monitoring that this process keeps moving. During your daily sweep: \- Check if any videos are sitting in “Submitted for QC” for too long. \- Check if any videos are stuck in the revision loop. \- Make sure Airtable statuses are being updated at each step. \- You can use the dedicated Airtable views for “Video Needs Checking (QC)” and “Editing Revisions” to quickly see what is in these stages.

### **Stage 8: Send the Video to the Client for Review**

**Who:** You **What happens:** Once the video is approved by the agency (status: “Approved by Agency”), you send it to the client for their review. This involves:

1. Go to the client’s Google Drive folder \- Finished Videos.

2. Create a new subfolder named with the video number (e.g., “62”).

3. Download the approved thumbnail from Ram’s Slack channel and upload it to this folder.

4. Add social posts to the folder (these are written by the scriptwriter \- they should be available by this point).

5. Add any other deliverables the client expects \- **check the Deliverables field in the client’s Airtable record** to see exactly what each client gets.

6. Send the client a message in their Slack channel using the message template (includes the Frame.io review link and the Drive folder link).

7. Update the Airtable editing status to “Sent to Client for Checking.”

8. Let the editor know the video has been sent to the client.

*Detailed instructions and the message template are in Part 8\.*

### **Stage 9: Client Review**

**Who:** Client \+ You (if revisions are needed) **What happens:** The client watches the video via the Frame.io link and either:

* **Approves it** \- they confirm in Slack. You update the Airtable editing status to **“Approved by Client”** and move to Stage 10\.

* **Requests revisions** \- they leave revision comments directly in Frame.io. When this happens:

  1. Update the Airtable editing status to “Client Sent Revisions.” This automatically pings the editor in Slack \- you do not need to message them separately.

  2. The editor implements the revisions and resubmits.

  3. You (or the QC, depending on the scope of changes) review the updated version.

  4. Send the updated version back to the client. Repeat until approved.

### **Stage 10: Schedule the Video on YouTube**

**Who:** You **What happens:** Once the client approves the video, you schedule it on their YouTube channel. This is a detailed process:

1. Download the final video file and the approved thumbnail (from Ram’s Slack channel \- download to your computer).

2. Log into the client’s YouTube channel.

3. Upload the video and configure: title (from script), description (from template), tags (using rapidtags.io), thumbnail, video elements, monetization settings (if applicable), and schedule time.

4. Standard posting time: **11:00 AM EST on weekdays.**

5. For clients with 4 videos/month, post weekly on the same day.

6. **Double-check everything** before finalizing \- right video file, matching thumbnail, working links in description, correct schedule time.

7. Update Airtable editing status to “Scheduled.”

8. Message the client in Slack confirming the scheduled date and time.

*Detailed step-by-step instructions are in Part 9\.*

# **Part 4: Assigning Videos in Airtable**

When a client records a video, your job is to get it into Airtable and assigned to an editor and thumbnail designer as quickly as possible. This is how the editing process kicks off \- nothing moves until you do this step.

---

## **How You Know a Client Has Recorded**

You will find out through Slack. Most clients post their recording link directly in their Slack channel with a message like “new vid” and the link. Some clients upload to their Google Drive raw footage folder instead, but they will still let you know in Slack that they uploaded it.

When you see a new recording come in, move to the assignment process below.

---

## **Step-by-Step: Assigning a Video**

### **Step 1: Find the Video Entry in Airtable**

In most cases, the Airtable entry for this video already exists because the scriptwriter created it during the scripting phase (it will have a status like “Script Sent to Client”). However, sometimes the video is not one that we scripted \- the client may have recorded something on their own, or it may be a special project. In those cases, the Airtable entry will not exist yet and you will need to create one.

Go to the **“Videos This Month (LF)”** view in Airtable. This is the view you should use for assignments. Scroll to the client and find the entry that matches the video they just recorded.

**How to match it to the correct script:** \- Check the script the client recorded. The scripts have numbers, and the Airtable entry will have the same video number. \- You can cross-reference by going to the client’s Google Drive scripts folder and matching the recording to the correct script. \- Make sure the recording actually matches the script \- confirm it is the right one before proceeding.

**If the entry does not exist yet** (the video was not one we scripted, or it is a special project): \- Create a new entry in Airtable. \- Set the client. \- Set the video number to the next number after the client’s last video (e.g., if their last video was 32, this one is 33).

### **Step 2: Fill in the Fields (In This Order)**

Once you have the right Airtable entry, fill in the fields in this order:

**1\. Assigned Editor** Select the editor who edits for this client. If you are not sure which editor handles which client, check with Samu.

**2\. Deadline** The standard turnaround is **3 days** from assignment.

Before setting the deadline, check the **“Videos by Editors”** view to see what the editor is currently working on: \- If the editor has no other active videos, set the deadline to 3 days from now. \- If the editor is currently finishing up another video (e.g., their current video’s deadline is today), they should be able to start this new one right away. Count 3 days from when they will realistically start. \- If the editor already has multiple active videos, you may need to set a longer deadline. Use your judgment based on their current workload.

**Example:** Today is the 14th. The editor has one video with a deadline of today, so she should be finishing up and can start the new one right away. Three days from now is the 17th, so set the deadline to the 17th.

**3\. Editing Status \- set to “Sent to Editor”** This is the step that triggers the automation. When you set the status to “Sent to Editor,” an automated Slack message is sent to the editor in their channel notifying them that a new video has been assigned.

**4\. Raw Footage** Paste the recording link from the client’s Slack message into the raw footage field.

**5\. Link to Script** This may already be filled in from the scripting phase. If not, paste the link to the script from the client’s Google Drive scripts folder.

**6\. Thumbnail Deadline** Always set this to **the next day** after assignment. We have a 24-hour turnaround for thumbnails. For example, if you are assigning on the 9th, set the thumbnail deadline to the 10th.

**7\. Thumbnail Status \- set to “New”** This triggers an automated notification to Ram (the thumbnail designer) so he knows there is a new thumbnail to create.

**8\. Client Notes** This is where you put any extra context the editor needs to know. Check the client’s Slack message for: \- Any notes or instructions about the video (e.g., “for the intro, use this clip” or “skip the first 2 minutes, that was a test take”). \- Any special requests or context. \- Any assets the editor should use (screenshots, links, reference videos).

If the client left notes, copy them into this field. Write it clearly so the editor can understand it without needing to go back to Slack.

Most of the time, clients just send a recording link with no extra notes. In that case, you can leave this field blank.

**Important \- multiple recording links:** The raw footage field only holds one link. If the client sent multiple files (e.g., a main recording plus a separate intro/outro file), put the main link in the raw footage field and paste the additional links in Client Notes. Write something like: “Additional recording files \- see links below” so the editor knows to check here.

## **Urgent Videos**

Occasionally, there will be a video that needs to be done as fast as possible \- faster than the standard 3-day turnaround. In these cases:

1. Set a shorter deadline (e.g., 1-2 days).

# **Part 6: Quality Checking Workflow**

The quality check (QC) is handled by a dedicated quality checker \- not by you. However, you need to understand how it works because you are responsible for monitoring that the QC process keeps moving and does not become a bottleneck.

---

## **How the QC Process Works**

We use **Frame.io** for hosting videos and giving revisions throughout the QC process. Editors upload their edited video to Frame.io and paste the Frame link in Airtable.

### **1\. Editor Submits the Video**

When an editor finishes editing a video, they: \- Upload the video to Frame.io. \- Paste the Frame.io link in the edited footage field in Airtable. \- Update the editing status to **“Submitted for QC.”** This automatically pings the quality checker.

You can see all videos waiting for QC in the **“Video Needs Checking (QC)”** Airtable view.

### **2\. Quality Checker Reviews and Gives Revisions**

The quality checker watches the video on Frame.io and leaves revision comments directly in Frame.io. The goal is to give **all revisions in this first round** so the editor can address everything at once. After leaving their notes, the quality checker updates the status to **“Editing Revisions.”** This automatically pings the editor in Slack.

### **3\. Editor Implements Revisions and Resubmits**

The editor implements all the changes based on the Frame.io comments and resubmits by updating the status back to **“Submitted for QC.”**

### **4\. Cross-Check**

The quality checker reviews the new version. Since all revisions were given in the first round, this second round is a cross-check to confirm everything was implemented correctly.

* **Most of the time,** the revisions have been properly implemented and the quality checker approves the video.

* **Sometimes,** the quality checker finds remaining issues and sets the status back to “Editing Revisions” again. The editor fixes those and resubmits. This cycle repeats until the quality checker is satisfied.

You can see all videos currently in the revision loop in the **“Editing Revisions”** Airtable view.

### **5\. Video Approved**

Once the quality checker is satisfied, they update the status to **“Approved by Agency.”** This means the video is ready to be sent to the client.

You can see all approved videos waiting to be sent in the **“Approved by Agency”** Airtable view. When you see a video here, your job is to send it to the client for review (covered in Part 8).

---

## **Your Role: Monitoring the QC Pipeline**

During your daily sweeps, check these two views:

**Video Needs Checking (QC):** \- Are there videos sitting here? How long have they been waiting? \- If a video has been waiting for QC for an unusually long time, flag it to the quality checker and to Samu.

**Editing Revisions:** \- Are there videos stuck in the revision loop? Check if editors are making progress on their revisions. \- If a video has been going back and forth for more than two rounds, be aware of it \- it may be affecting the deadline. \- If the revision loop is causing a deadline to be missed, update the deadline in Airtable and communicate with the editor.

**Approved by Agency:** \- Are there videos here that you have not yet sent to the client? If so, that is your action item \- send them as soon as possible.

Sometimes the title does not give away everything, so watching the intro helps you write an accurate description. \- Make sure all names are spelled correctly \- copy-paste names rather than typing them to avoid errors.

**UTM links:** Some clients have UTM tracking links in their descriptions. These can be different for each client. If the description template explains how to set them up, follow that. If not, look at the client’s previous videos on YouTube to see how UTM links were formatted and customize accordingly. Make sure you update parameters like episode number or guest name so the tracking is accurate.

**Links:** All links in the description must include https:// at the beginning. Without it, YouTube will not make them clickable. Double-check that every link starts with https:// and is fully written out.

### **Step 8: Video Elements**

Import video elements from the client’s last uploaded video: 1\. Click “Import from video.” 2\. Select the most recent video on the channel. 3\. This will pull in the end screen elements (recommended video card, subscribe button, etc.).

**Check for call-to-actions:** Play the outro of the video. If the speaker recommends a specific video at the end (e.g., “if you want to learn about XYZ, watch this video”), you need to: \- Change the recommended video element to point to that specific video. \- Position it on the correct side of the screen (where they point or where it makes sense visually).

If there is no specific call-to-action, just keep the default elements from the import.

You can also look at how previous videos were set up for this client to stay consistent.

### **Step 9: Monetization (If Applicable)**

Some client channels are monetized, some are not. If the channel is monetized, go to the monetization section and approve monetization for the video. This is straightforward \- just select the appropriate options.

If the channel is not monetized, skip this step.

### **Step 10: Schedule the Video**

Set the video to **Schedule** (not Public \- never set it to Public directly).

**Standard posting time: 11:00 AM EST on weekdays.** Make sure you convert this to your local time zone when setting it.

**Which day to schedule:** \- For clients with 4 videos per month: post weekly on the same day. Check the client’s last uploaded video to see which day of the week they post, and schedule this one for the same day next week. \- If we are behind schedule: post at the soonest weekday at 11 AM EST. \- Check the client’s Slack channel for any special posting requests (some clients want specific days or times). \- If you are unsure about when to schedule, ask Samu.

### **Step 11: Double-Check Everything (Critical)**

This is the most important step. Mistakes in scheduling are visible to the client’s audience and can damage the client relationship. Go through this checklist:

**Video file:** \- Play the intro and skim through the video to confirm it is the right video. \- Skim to the end to make sure there is no black screen or blank portion at the end. We have had this happen before \- a black screen in the last minute of a video. Always check.

**Thumbnail:** \- Confirm the thumbnail matches the title and the video content. \- Make sure it is the right thumbnail for this video (not a different video or client).

**Description:** \- Read through the full description. \- Check for spelling errors. \- Click every link to make sure they work and go to the right place. \- Make sure all links include https:// and are clickable. \- Make sure all names and customized sections are correct.

**Schedule:** \- Confirm the video is set to Schedule (not Public). \- Confirm the date and time are correct (11 AM EST on the right weekday).

**Tags:** \- Quickly scan to confirm all tags are relevant and none are off-topic.

### **Step 12: Update Airtable**

Set the editing status to **“Scheduled.”**

### **Step 13: Message the Client**

Send a message in the client’s Slack channel:

---

**Message template:**

Hey @\[client name\], new video is scheduled for \[day\] 11am EST: \[YouTube video link\]

---

If it is a team channel rather than an individual, you can say “Hey guys” instead of tagging one person. If the posting time is different from 11 AM EST for any reason, update the time in the message.

---

## **A Note on This Process**

Scheduling is one of the most critical parts of the job because what you upload goes directly to the client’s audience. We are not allowed to make mistakes here. If at any point during this process you have a question, something is unclear, or you are unsure about any detail \- always ask Samu before proceeding. It is better to ask and get it right than to guess and make an error that is visible to the public.

---

## **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

* Setting the video to Public instead of Schedule. Always use Schedule.

* Forgetting to replace title variables (like “X minutes”).

* Leaving the previous video’s guest name or episode number in the description.

* Links in the description missing https:// (they will not be clickable).

* Uploading the wrong thumbnail (for a different video or client).

* Not checking the end of the video for black screens.

* Closing the browser window before the upload finishes.

* Scheduling on a weekend instead of a weekday.

* Scheduling at the wrong time (forgetting to convert to EST).

## **The 20-Minute Rule**

This was covered in Part 5 but it is worth repeating here because it is central to client communication. During your work hours, respond to every client message within 20 minutes. If you cannot fully resolve something in 20 minutes, acknowledge the message and let the client know you are on it.

Hey @\[NAME\], great to meet you\! I’m the ops manager at KS Media and I’m excited to start working together. Samu will be the main point of contact, but I’ll help wherever I can.

---

Make sure you tag the client with @ \- do not just type their name as plain text. This same message is used for all clients, whether they are new or have been working with us for a while.

---

## **Day-to-Day Client Communication**

### **What You Handle Independently**

Once you are ramped up, you can handle these types of client communications on your own: \- Confirming you received a recording: “Got it, thanks\! We’ll get started on this right away.” \- Sending videos for review (using the template from Part 8). \- Confirming a video is scheduled (using the template from Part 9). \- Following up on recordings: “Hey \[name\], just checking in \- do you have an ETA on when you’ll be able to record the next video?” \- Following up on video reviews: “Hey \[name\], just checking in on the video we sent over \- any feedback or is it good to go?” \- Answering straightforward questions about timelines, scheduling, or deliverables.

### **What You Escalate to Samu**

Always check with Samu before responding to clients about: \- Strategy or creative direction (video topics, content approach, channel strategy). \- Pricing, contracts, or billing. \- Client-specific preferences you are not familiar with yet. \- Complaints or anything where the client seems unhappy. \- Anything you are unsure about.

When escalating, message Samu with the context and what you think the response should be. Let Samu approve or adjust before you send it to the client.

### **During Your First Few Days**

Check with Samu before responding to any client message. This is how you learn the tone, expectations, and client-specific nuances. Once Samu is confident you understand each client’s situation, you will handle most communication independently.

---

## **Common Client Scenarios**

### **Client Sends a Recording**

* Acknowledge it in the client channel within 20 minutes.

* Assign it in Airtable (Part 4).

* Update Samu.

### **Client Approves a Video**

* Thank them.

* Update Airtable to “Approved by Client.”

* Schedule the video (Part 9).

### **Client Sends Revisions**

* Acknowledge it: “Got it, we’ll get these implemented\!”

* Update Airtable to “Client Sent Revisions” (auto-pings editor).

* Monitor the editor’s progress on the revisions.

### **Client Asks About a Video’s Status**

* Check Airtable for the current status.

* **Keep your response broad and safe.** We always want to give super safe ETAs and expectations so we never end up in a situation where we cannot deliver on what we said. Use language like: “We’re currently wrapping it up and will be sending it your way soon\!” Avoid giving specific dates unless the client specifically asks for one.

* **If the client specifically asks for a date:** Only give a date you are 100% sure the editor will finish by. Best practice is to take when you actually expect it to be fully done and add one or two extra days on top, just to be safe. For example, if you expect it to be done by Wednesday, tell the client Friday.

* **If there is a tight deadline the client needs it by**, or you are not sure exactly what to say, ask Samu before responding to the client.

* Never make promises about timelines you are not confident in.

### **Client Asks a Question You Cannot Answer**

* Acknowledge the message within 20 minutes: “Will let @Samu get back to you on this”

* Let Samu know in your direct channel with him so he can respond.

### **Client Has Not Recorded in a While**

* Send a friendly follow-up: “Hey \[name\], just checking in \- do you have an ETA on when you’ll be able to record the next video?”

* If no response after a few days, follow up again.

* If it continues, let Samu know.

---

## **Tone and Style**

* Be friendly and professional. Keep messages concise.

* Use the client’s first name.

* Always tag clients with @ when addressing them.

* Do not over-explain or write long paragraphs \- keep it simple and direct.

* Match the energy of the client. If they are casual, you can be casual. If they are more formal, be more formal.

* Never make promises about timelines you are not confident in. When in doubt, keep it broad or check with Samu first.
